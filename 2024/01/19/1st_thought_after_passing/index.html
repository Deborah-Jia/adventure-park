Passing CFA Level II: Effort, Environment, and the Right Track

Date: January 19, 2024  Friday Category: Slices of Life

These past two days, I am still in the process of digesting the news that I PASSED. I passed on my first attempt, surpassing all other candidates, which came as a complete surprise. During and after the exam, I was deeply disappointed with my performance and was convinced I would definitely fail. I was only pretending to be strong to give myself some psychological comfort.

But that wasn’t the case. I passed, and I accepted everyone’s congratulations, kind words, and even jealousy. Why did I pass? Was it because the exam was all multiple-choice questions and I was just lucky? No. I passed with a strong score: two subjects with full marks and six above 70%. Then why was I still so disappointed and so harsh on myself?

Because I have always lived in a harsh world with intense competition. I was trained to believe that nobody remembers second place, and that you must always push yourself to win and impress others. I enjoyed two days of rest after I joined the top firm, and the last time I had anything similar was in kindergarten. In primary and middle school, I had to attend after-school classes. In high school, I had only one day of rest each month, spent doing laundry and buying daily necessities. I cut my wrist and forced myself not to eat as punishment whenever I didn’t get a satisfactory exam score.

I did my best and became completely exhausted throughout my three years of high school, with no TV or internet for relaxation. I achieved a score good enough to get into a top 10–15 university in China, in almost any program I wanted. But I ended up in a top 200 university. Why?

The enrollment system is flawed and unfair. I had only one plan, and I fell off the list. Some people were simply lucky and got into top universities with much lower scores than mine. As a first-generation college student in my family, no one knew what college was like. I had no real choice but to go. Dropping out and retaking the national exam was never an option.

Then my life completely changed—arguably, deteriorated.

An average school offers average education and average peers. I was verbally attacked simply because I was smarter than others, which made them feel threatened. My English was even better than the teachers’; one of them often asked me to translate the English textbooks, which only made other students feel worse. I was exhausted. After graduation, I couldn’t find a job.

I used to think this was just life—mine and everyone else’s. Unfair, but something you have to accept. In 2016, the year I graduated, I failed all three major exams: the national entrance exam for a master’s degree, the interpreting certificate exam, and the certified accountant exam. I almost accepted that I wasn’t as smart as I thought I was. Then came 2020. I passed IELTS and GMAT, and then CFA Level I with a global top 10% result.

Why did life suddenly become easier? Why could I suddenly achieve so much? Simply because I changed my track. I am done competing with Chinese candidates.

So it was never that I wasn’t hardworking, or that the exams were too difficult. It was that the competition was fierce and I was never in a privileged position. Your efforts do pay off—as long as you are in the right place.

That’s why I became curious about how far I can go by pushing myself further and seeing whether I can pass Level III in a less painful way. If the environment is fair, I am not afraid of the pain of hard work. The pain of regret hurts more. My life is already ten times easier than that of my 16-year-old high school self.
