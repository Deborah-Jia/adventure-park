5. Probabilityᡣ𐭩
  1. Conditional Probability: the probabilty of an event A given that event B has occurred. e.g. Patient having a paticular disease given they tested positive.

Bayes’ rule:

Note: in such questions, it is important to list each probability properly according to the question conditions.

  1. Law of Total Probability: decompose the probability of event B happening into the weighted sum of conditional probabilities based on each possible scenario having occurred.

  1. Counting: Use permutations when order matters and combinations when it doesn’t.

  1. Random Variables and Probability Distribution

Discrete random variables have a countable range and are described by the probability mass function (PMF).

Continuous random variables have an uncountable range and are described by the probability density function (PDF), which is not measurable for a specific value of xxx.

The cumulative distribution function (CDF) is either given by a sum or an integral.

  1. Joint, Marginal, and Conditional Probability Distributions

  2. Probability Distributions

    1. Binomial distribution: the probability of k number of successes in n independent trials where the outcome of each event is binary, e.g. coin flips.

    2. Poisson distribution: the probability of the number of events occuring within a particular fixed interval where the known, constant rate of each event’s occurrence is λ.

      Both the mean and the variance of a Poisson distribution are λ.

      Example: assessing counts over a continuous interval such as the number of visits to a website in a certain period of time or the number of defects in a square foot of fabric.

    3. Uniform distribution: a constant probability of an X falling between values on the interval a to b.

      The mean is (a+b)/2, the variance is [(b-a)^2]/12

      Example: sampling (random number generation) and hypotesis testing

    4. Exponential distribution: the probability distribution of the distance between events in a Poisson point process having a set rate parameter of λ.

    ##### Basic Financial Products Knowledge

    1. Can you explain the key differences between equities, fixed income, and derivatives?

    - Follow-up: How do these products behave differently in a portfolio context?

    1. What are the main types of fixed income securities, and how do they differ in terms of risk and return?

    - Follow-up: How would you assess the credit risk of a corporate bond?

    1. What is the difference between active and passive investment strategies?

    - Follow-up: How does BlackRock’s approach to ETFs (iShares) fit into this framework?

    1. Can you describe the concept of duration in fixed income? How does it impact a bond’s price sensitivity to interest rate changes?
    2. What are the key characteristics of a derivative product like an option or a swap?

    - Follow-up: How might derivatives be used to hedge portfolio risk?

    1. What is the difference between mutual funds and ETFs?

    - Follow-up: How do these products align with BlackRock’s investment philosophy?

    1. How do you evaluate the risk-return profile of a portfolio that includes a mix of asset classes (e.g., equities, bonds, and alternatives)?

    Performance and Attributionᡣ𐭩
    1. What is performance attribution, and why is it important in portfolio management?

    - Follow-up: Can you explain the difference between allocation effect and selection effect in attribution analysis?

    1. How would you measure the contribution of a specific asset class to the overall portfolio performance?
    2. What are the key metrics used to evaluate portfolio performance (e.g., Sharpe ratio, alpha, beta)?

    - Follow-up: How would you interpret a negative alpha for a portfolio?

    1. Can you explain the concept of tracking error? How is it relevant to passive and active strategies?
    2. What is the difference between absolute and relative performance? How would you communicate this to a client?

    —

    Portfolio Risk Modellingᡣ𐭩
    1. What are the main types of risk in a portfolio (e.g., market risk, credit risk, liquidity risk)?

    - Follow-up: How would you model and quantify these risks?

    1. Can you explain Value at Risk (VaR) and its limitations as a risk measure?
    2. How would you assess the impact of interest rate changes on a fixed income portfolio?
    3. What is stress testing, and how does it complement traditional risk models?
    4. How do you incorporate macroeconomic factors into portfolio risk modelling?
    5. What is the role of correlation in portfolio risk, and how can diversification reduce risk?

    —
