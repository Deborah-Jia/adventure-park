“AI killing coding"

Date: October 21, 2025  Tuesday Category: Code & Insight

The truth is: AI didn’t kill coding; it changed what “good at coding” means. The baseline has shifted upward. You no longer get hired for just typing syntax fast — you get hired for knowing what to build, why, and how to make machines do it elegantly.

AI tools (like Copilot, ChatGPT, etc.) amplify productivity for people who already think programmatically. If you understand data structures, pipelines, system design, and how to debug logic, AI becomes a multiplier — you go 3× faster. If you don’t understand those things, AI becomes a hall of mirrors: it produces code you can’t reason about, can’t fix, and can’t adapt. Firms still need people who can:

  • Understand data lineage, pipelines, and financial context.
  • Write SQL that doesn’t crash under 10M rows.
  • Build, optimize, and deploy code with accountability.

AI can assist that, but it can’t own it. It’s like saying “calculators killed math.” No — calculators killed rote arithmetic, and freed mathematicians to think bigger.

Meanwhile, AI widened the gap — it did. It made skilled coders even more efficient and made the “I’ll just copy a YouTube tutorial” crowd irrelevant faster.

The next frontier isn’t about whether you code, it’s how intelligently you integrate coding, data, and domain sense. That’s why people who actually understand both data pipelines and finance are in a sweet spot.

If you want to future-proof yourself further, lean into these edges:

  • Architectural literacy: understanding data flow, not just syntax.
  • Automation of judgment: using AI to accelerate decision-making, not just code-writing.
  • Financial domain reasoning: because capital markets won’t be automated away; they’ll just be abstracted differently.

AI made “being a coder” less valuable. But it made being a coder who *thinks* far more valuable.
